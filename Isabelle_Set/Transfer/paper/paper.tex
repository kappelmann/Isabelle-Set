\section{Lifting Triples}
Recall the goal of lifting:
given a value on the representation level and a transfer relation,
generate an abstract value related to it.
To do so, we must use suitable abstraction and representation functions,
mapping back and forth between related values.
We associate such functions with transfer relations by using \emph{lifting-triples},
which are similar to quotient predicates used by the lifting tool.
Specifically, a lifting triple,
written \(\langle T, abs, rep \rangle\),
combines a transfer relation \(T : \alpha \Rightarrow \beta \Rightarrow \bool\) with an abstraction function \(abs : \alpha \Rightarrow \beta\) and representation function \(rep : \beta \Rightarrow \alpha\) respecting the transfer relation.
\kevin{I rewrote this paragraph a bit; you may double check it}
\begin{definition}[Lifting triple]\label{def-lifting-triple}
	A transfer relation \(T : \alpha \Rightarrow \beta \Rightarrow \bool\) and functions \(abs : \alpha \Rightarrow \beta\) and \(rep : \beta \Rightarrow \alpha\) form a lifting triple, written \(\langle T, abs, rep \rangle\), if
	\begin{align*}
		\text{1.}\quad&\forall x\ldotp \indom\ T\ x \longrightarrow T\ x\ (abs\ x) \\
		\text{2.}\quad&\forall y\ldotp \incodom\ T\ y \longrightarrow T\ (rep\ y)\ y
	\end{align*}
\end{definition}
When comparing this definition to the quotient predicate from [source], two differences stand out: First, \(\eqabs\ T\) and \(\eqrep\ T\) are not included in the lifting triple since they can unambiguously be defined in terms of the transfer relation. Second, the relation in a quotient-predicate must be right-unique and right-total, while the relation in a lifting-triple must only satisfy the Z-property, as explained in \kevin{cleveref} Section \ref{sec-prelims}.
This generalization has the benefit of being symmetric:
any lifting triple gives rise to a dual lifting triple.
\begin{lemma}[Dual lifting triple]\label{dual-lifting-triple}
  If $\langle T, abs, rep \rangle$ then $\langle T\inv, rep, abs \rangle$.
\end{lemma}

If we want to express transfer relations between representations and abstractions of abstract data types, like like vectors and polynomials,
we need the relational analogue of type constructors.
These are known as \emph{parameterized transfer relations} [source].
Just like type constructors map types to types,
parameterized transfer relations map relations to relations.
When \(\kappa\) and \(\kappa'\) are type constructs of arity \(n\),
a transfer relation between \(\kappa\ \overline{\alpha}\) and \(\kappa'\ \overline{\alpha'}\)
is composed of two parts:

First, a relator \(rel\) of type
\begin{equation*}
(\alpha_1 \Rightarrow \alpha'_1 \Rightarrow \bool) \Rightarrow \ldots \Rightarrow (\alpha_n \Rightarrow \alpha'_n \Rightarrow \bool) \Rightarrow \kappa\ \overline{\alpha} \Rightarrow \kappa\ \overline{\alpha'} \Rightarrow \bool
\end{equation*}
which takes relations on the type parameters as arguments
and lifts them to a relation on \(\kappa\).
Second, a non-parameterized transfer relation \(T : \kappa\ \overline{\alpha} \Rightarrow \kappa'\ \overline{\alpha} \Rightarrow \bool\) that relates representation and abstraction values on the outer level.

For the class of transfer relations supported by the lifting tool,
the composition of these components always resulted in a transfer relation.
This does not hold for our generalized transfer relations
since the Z-property is not closed under composition.
We can, however, prove a restricted composition theorem:
\begin{lemma}[Composition of transfer relations]\label{comp-of-trans-rels}
The composition of two transfer relations \(T_1\) and \(T_2\) is again a transfer relation if \(\eqabs\ T_1\) commutes with \( \eqrep\ T_2 \) under relation composition.
Formally: if \(\eqabs\ T_1 \relcomp \eqrep\ T_2 = \eqrep\ T_2 \relcomp \eqabs\ T_1\) then \(T_1 \ \relcomp T_2\) is a transfer relation.
\end{lemma}
When applying this theorem to build a transfer relation between \(\kappa\ \overline{\alpha}\) and \(\kappa'\ \overline{\alpha'}\),
the side condition becomes
\begin{equation*}
\eqabs\ (rel\ T_1 \ldots T_n) \relcomp \eqrep\ T = \eqrep\ T \relcomp \eqabs\ (rel\ T_1 \ldots T_n).
\end{equation*}
This intuitively means that the representation of the type constructor
\(\kappa\) is independent of the representations of the type arguments.
Since it is reasonable to expect this property for all meaningful datatypes, the side-condition should not lead to any practical limitations. In particular, the theorem is applicable to all datatypes which can be handled by the lifting tool
because there \(\eqabs\ (rel\ T_1 \ldots T_n)\) is the equality relation,
which commutes with all relations.

In particular when working with algebraic structures, one often wants to extend one structure (eg. \(\nat\)) to a larger structure (eg. \(\inte\)). However when using standard constructions of \(\inte\) like a quotient of \(\nat \times \nat\) or a sum of \(\nat\) and \(\nat_+\), one faces the problem that \(\nat\) is not a subset of \(\inte\). Set extensions solve this problem by constructing a new set as the union of \(\nat\) and all elements in the representation set of \(\inte\) which do not represent a value already included in \(\nat\). To formalize this, we first need a definition of bijections between two sets.

\begin{definition}[Bijection]
	\begin{equation}
		\begin{split}
			\bijection\ A\ B\ f\ g := & (\forall x \in A\ldotp f\ x \in B \land g\ (f\ x) = x) \land \\
			& (\forall y \in B\ldotp g\ y \in A \land f\ (g\ y) = y)
		\end{split}
	\end{equation}
\end{definition}

Given sets \(A\) and \(B\) and an injection \(f\) from \(A\) to \(B\), we want to construct a set \(B'\) and functions \(abs : [B] \rightarrow [B']\) and \(rep : [B'] \rightarrow [B]\) st. \(\bijection\ B\ B'\ abs\ rep\), \(A \subseteq B'\) and \(\forall x \in A\ldotp abs\ x = f\ x\). The last property states that \(abs\) and \(f\) are identical when their domains are restricted to \(A\) ie. the embedding of \(A\) in \(B'\) by \(abs\) respects the structural connection between \(A\) and \(B\) given by \(f\). This is equivalent to \(\forall x \in A\ldotp abs\ (f\ x) = x\).

[Using the function relator, the last two properties could be expressed more elegantly as \((\eq\ A \Rrightarrow \eq\ B')\ abs\ f\) and \((\eq\ A \Rrightarrow \eq\ A)\ (abs \circ f)\ \id\), but I haven't defined it at the point I wrote this part originally.]

To obtain such a bijection, we define
\begin{align}
	B' &:= A \cup \{(A, x) \mid x \in (B \setminus \{f\ a \mid a \in A\})\} \\
	rep\ y &:= f\ x\ \text{if}\ y \in A\ \text{else}\ \snd\ y \\
	abs\ x &:= z\ \text{st.}\ x \in A \land f\ x = x\ \text{if}\ \exists z \in A\ldotp f\ z = x\ \text{else}\ (A, x)
\end{align}
where \( (x, y) \) is the pair containing \(x\) and \(y\) and \(\snd\ (x, y) = y\).

That these definitions satisfy the properties stated above was proven in Isabelle/HOL by [?]. It is, however, not convenient to directly define operations on the set \(B'\) since such definitions would require case distinctions between elements which are contained in the extended set and those which are not. Instead, an operation on \(B'\) is usually defined as a combination of the respective operation on \(B\) and the functions \(abs\) and \(rep\) which translate between \(B\) and \(B'\).

While arbitrary lifting triples can be constructed from scratch, in the context of lifting operations to types constructed by set extensions, we only need lifting triples build from bijections and the lifting of these lifting triples over type constructors.

\begin{lemma}[Lifting triple from bijection]
	If \(\bijection\ A\ B\ f\ g \) then
	\begin{equation}
		\langle \birel\ A\ f, f, g \rangle
	\end{equation}
	where
	\begin{equation}
		\birel\ A\ f\ x\ y := x \in A \land f\ x = y
	\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

In particular, we can construct a lifting triple from any set-extension.

\begin{example}[Lifting triple from set extensions]\label{lifting-int}

To fix this, we can build the set extension of \(\nat\) by \(\inte_{\rep}\) with the injection \(\mathsf{in_l}\) from \(\nat\) to \(\inte_{\rep}\). When the bijection theorem of this set extension is \(\bijection\ \inte_{\rep}\ \inte\ \abs_\inte\ \rep_\inte\), the corresponding transfer triple is \(\langle \relint, \abs_\inte, \rep_\inte \rangle\) where \(\relint := \birel\ \inte_{\rep}\ \abs_\inte\). In particular, \(\relint\ (\mathsf{in_l}\ n)\ n\) for any \(n \in \nat\).
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Parameterized lifting triple for algebraic data types]
	Given two transfer relations \(T_1 : \alpha \Rightarrow \gamma\) and \(T_2 : \beta \Rightarrow \delta\), values of the product types \(\alpha \times \beta\) and \(\gamma \times \delta\) are related if both components are related, values of the sum types \(\alpha + \beta\) and \(\gamma + \delta\) are related if both are left values or both are right values and their content is related and finally tow values of the list types \(\alpha^\ast\) and \(\gamma^\ast\) are related if they have the same length and their elements are related pointwise. Formally, the parameterized lifting triple for the product, sum and list types are
	\begin{equation*}
		\langle T_1, abs_1, rep_1 \rangle \land \langle T_2, abs_2, rep_2 \rangle \longrightarrow \langle \relprod\ T_1\ T_2, \mapprod\ abs_1\ abs_2, \mapprod\ rep_1\ rep_2 \rangle
	\end{equation*}
	\begin{equation*}
		\langle T_1, abs_1, rep_1 \rangle \land \langle T_2, abs_2, rep_2 \rangle \longrightarrow \langle \relsum\ T_1\ T_2, \mapsum\ abs_1\ abs_2, \mapsum\ rep_1\ rep_2 \rangle
	\end{equation*}
	and
	\begin{equation*}
		\langle T, abs, rep \rangle \longrightarrow \langle \rellist\ T, \maplist\ abs, \maplist\ rep \rangle
	\end{equation*}
	resp. where
	\begin{align*}
		\mapprod\ f\ g\ (x, y) &:= (f\ x, g\ y) \\
		\relprod\ T_1\ T_2\ (x, y)\ (x', y') &:= T_1\ x\ x' \land T_2\ y\ y' \\
		\mapsum\ f\ g\ (\Left\ x) &:= \Left\ (f\ x) \\
		\mapsum\ f\ g\ (\Right\ y) &:= \Right\ (f\ y) \\
		\relsum\ T_1\ T_2\ (\Left\ x)\ (\Left\ x') &:= T_1\ x\ x' \\
		\relsum\ T_1\ T_2\ (\Right\ y)\ (\Right\ y') &:= T_2\ y\ y' \\
		\relsum\ T_1\ T_2\ \_\ \_ &:= \false \\
		\maplist\ f\ [] &:= [] \\
		\maplist\ f\ (x \cons xs) &:= f\ x \cons \maplist\ f\ xs \\
		\rellist\ T\ []\ [] &:= \true \\
		\rellist\ T\ (x \cons xs)\ (y \cons ys) &:= T\ x\ y \land \rellist\ T\ xs\ ys \\
		\rellist\ T\ \_\ \_ &:= \false \\
	\end{align*}
\end{example}

\begin{lemma}[Parameterized lifting triple for functions]\label{fun-lifing-triple}
	Functions are related if they map related arguments to related results. Hence, the parameterized lifting triple for the function type is
	\begin{equation*}
		\langle T_1, abs_1, rep_1 \rangle \land \langle T_2, abs_2, rep_2 \rangle \longrightarrow \langle \relfun\ T_1\ T_2, \mapfun\ rep_1\ abs_2 , \mapfun\ abs_1\ rep_2 \rangle
	\end{equation*}
	where
	\begin{align*}
		\mapfun\ f\ g\ h &:= g \circ h \circ f \\
		\relfun\ T_1\ T_2\ f\ g &:= \forall x\ y\ldotp T_1\ x\ y \longrightarrow T_2\ (f\ x)\ (g\ y)
	\end{align*}

	Depending on which notation is more convenient, I will write \(T_1 \Rrightarrow T_2\) for \(\relfun\ T_1\ T_2\) in the remainder of this paper. The relator \((\Rrightarrow)\) is right associative, so \(T_1 \Rrightarrow T_2 \Rrightarrow T_3\) means \(T_1 \Rrightarrow (T_2 \Rrightarrow T_3)\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{example}[Lifting triple for functions over \(\inte\)]
	Using the definitions of \(\inte_{\rep}\) and \(\inte\) from example \ref{lifting-int}, we can define the multiplication function on integers on the representation level \(\mathsf{mul_{\inte_{\rep}}} : [\inte_{\rep}] \Rightarrow [\inte_{\rep}] \Rightarrow [\inte_{\rep}]\) using operations on \(\nat\). I omit the definition here. The transfer relation we need to lift this function to \(\inte\) is \(\relfun\ \relint\ (\relfun\ \relint\ \relint)\) and the corresponding transfer triple is
	\begin{align*}
		\langle \relint \Rrightarrow \relint \Rrightarrow \relint,\\
		\mapfun\ \rep_\inte\ (\mapfun\ \rep_\inte\ \abs_\inte),\\
		\mapfun\ \abs_\inte\ (\mapfun\ \abs_\inte\ \rep_\inte) \rangle
	\end{align*}
	Now we can construct the multiplication on \(\inte\) as the lifting of \(\mathsf{mul_{\inte_{\rep}}}\):
	\begin{equation*}
		\mathsf{mul_\inte} := \mapfun\ \rep_\inte\ (\mapfun\ \rep_\inte\ \abs_\inte)\ \mathsf{mul_{\inte_{\rep}}}
	\end{equation*}
	From definition \ref{def-lifting-triple} we know that these two multiplication functions are related by the transfer relation stated above.

	Unfolding the definition of \(\mapfun\), we get
	\begin{equation*}
		\mathsf{mul_\inte}\ i\ j = \abs_\inte\ (\mathsf{mul_{\inte_{\rep}}}\ (\rep_\inte\ i)\ (\rep_\inte\ j))
	\end{equation*}
	as expected.

	Also functions with mixed argument and return types can be lifted. For instance, the divides predicate on integers, which return \(\true\) iff its first argument divides its second, and the power function on integers, which computes the first arguments raised to the power of the second, can be lifted with the transfer relations
	\begin{equation*}
		\relint \Rrightarrow \relint \Rrightarrow (\eq\ \univ_{\mathsf{bool}})
	\end{equation*}
	and
	\begin{equation*}
		\relint \Rrightarrow (\eq\ \nat) \Rrightarrow \relint
	\end{equation*}
	resp.
\end{example}

\begin{lemma}[lifting triple with dependent transfer relation for functions]
	Similar to how the type of functions can be generalized to dependent function types, the above example can be generalized to dependent transfer relations.

	Given a lifting triple
	\begin{equation*}
		\langle T_1, abs_1, rep_1 \rangle
	\end{equation*}
	and a parameterized lifting triple
	\begin{equation*}
		\forall x\ y \ldotp T_1\ x\ y \longrightarrow \langle T_2\ x\ y, abs_2\ y\ x, rep_2\ x\ y\rangle
	\end{equation*}
	if \(T_2\ x\ y\) does not depend on which representatives \(x\) and \(y\) are chosen from any given equivalence class of \(\eqrep\ T_1\) and \(\eqabs\ T_1\), reps. and \(abs_2\) and \(rep_2\) behave equivalently for equivalent arguments, formally:

		\forall x\ x'\ y\ y' \ldotp\, & T_1\ x\ y \land \eqrep\ T_1\ x\ x' \land \eqabs\ T_1\ y\ y' \longrightarrow \\
		& ((\eqabs\ (T_2\ x\ y)) \Rrightarrow (\eqrep\ (T_2\ x\ y)))\ (rep_2\ x\ y)\ (rep_2\ x'\ y') \\
		\forall x\ x'\ y\ y' \ldotp\, & T_1\ x\ y \land \eqrep\ T_1\ x\ x' \land \eqabs\ T_1\ y\ y' \longrightarrow \\
		& ((\eqrep\ (T_2\ x\ y)) \Rrightarrow (\eqabs\ (T_2\ x\ y)))\ (abs_2\ y\ x)\ (abs_2\ y'\ x')
	\end{align*}
	[these conditions could be expressed in a purely relational way without explicit quantifiers and the second argument could be dropped using \(\indom\)] \\
	then
	\begin{equation*}
		\langle \relfund\ T_1\ T_2, \mapfund\ rep_1\ abs_2, \mapfund\ abs_1\ rep_2 \rangle

\begin{example}[Lifting integer division]
	While the non-dependent function relation introduced in Lemma \ref{fun-lifing-triple} was sufficient to relate the multiplication and divides functions on the representation and abstraction level, this is not possible for all functions. For instance, implementations of the integer division function may be undefined or produce arbitrary results for some pairs of integers. Hence two implementations of this function do not need to return related values for all related pairs of arguments, but only for those where the first argument is divisible by the second. To express this relation, we need the more general dependent transfer relation for function defined above. Specifically, given an implementation of inter division over \(\inte_{\rep}\), we want to lift this function to \(\inte\). These two functions must be related by the transfer relation
	\begin{equation*}
		[\_\ i :: \relint] \Rrightarrow \reland\ \relint\ (\appabs\ (\mathsf{divides_\inte}\ i)) \Rrightarrow \relint
	\end{equation*}
	where
	\begin{align*}
		\appabs\ f\ \_\ y &:= f\ y \\
		& \text{alternatively: } \lambda f\ldotp \maprel\ f\ \mathsf{\pi_{abs}} \\
		& \text{where } \mathsf{\pi_{abs}}\ \_\ y := y \\
		\reland\ R\ S &:= (\maprel\ (\land)\ R)\ \aprel\ S \\
		\maprel\ f\ R\ x\ y &:= f\ (R\ x\ y) \\
		\aprel\ R\ S\ x\ y &:= R\ x\ y\ (S\ x\ y)
	\end{align*}
\end{example}
